## Overview

The Nuppeppō (ぬっぺっぽう) is a yokai of Japanese folklore depicted as an amorphous, wrinkled blob of flesh — roughly humanoid in scale but lacking distinct features beyond rudimentary fingers, toes, and a face suggested by folds of skin. Tradition assigns it no eyes or ears, and it is generally treated as harmless, wandering deserted places: abandoned temples, graveyards, and empty village streets, especially at night and at year's end. Its name derives from nupperi (ぬっぺり), a derogatory term for women wearing heavy makeup, referencing the saggy face. The Nuppeppō is depicted in major Edo-period yokai scrolls including the Bakemono no e (c. 1700, held at Brigham Young University's L. Tom Perry Special Collections), Sawaki Suushi's Hyakkai Zukan (1737), and Toriyama Sekien's Gazu Hyakki Yagyō (1776). A related tradition — the nikujin ('meat-person') reported at Sunpu Castle in 1609 — is sometimes associated with the Nuppeppō; in some lore, eating its flesh confers immortality, echoing Chinese 'feng' or panacea-creature traditions.
